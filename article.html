What a Niagara Cabernet Franc block reveals at 970 nanometers.

ESSAY 6 MIN READ APRIL 2026 TOM RIVERA

It was the second week of July. The Niagara Escarpment was dry — dryer than it had been that early in ten years — and the grower I was standing with walked me down a row of Cabernet Franc that looked, to the naked eye, perfect. Uniform canopy. Good leaf posture. Tan bark. Nothing wrong.

We had a report from that morning's satellite pass open on the tablet. And the tablet was telling a different story.

What the 970nm band actually measures

Water molecules absorb strongly at around 970 nanometers — deep in the near-infrared, well beyond anything your eye can register. When a leaf is fully hydrated, the 970nm trough in its reflectance curve is a sharp, clear dip. When water content drops, that trough fills in: the dip flattens out, not because the leaf is sending out a signal, but because there's simply less water molecule to do the absorbing.

"You can't look at a vine and know it's thirsty. You can only look at the reflectance curve and know that it's stopped being able to lie."

In our pipeline, 970nm is one of four anchor bands we pull from every panel on every pass. We normalize it against the plateau around 850nm (which tracks canopy structure, not water) and compute a per-panel Water Index. A healthy, hydrated vine under Niagara summer conditions sits between 0.12 and 0.18 on this index.

That block's Water Index values, that morning:

  • Row 1–8: 0.14 (normal)
  • Row 9–14: 0.09 (climbing into stress)
  • Row 15–22: 0.06 (significant stress)
  • Row 23–30: 0.13 (normal)

Why rows 15–22 and nowhere else

We walked them. And of course once you know where to look, the vines tell you. Slightly thinner lateral growth. Leaf angles very, very subtly steeper. A tiny curl at the edge of the fourth-node leaves. A scout walking normally would have picked these up in another ten to fourteen days — maybe a little sooner if they were having a good day. But not that morning. We had caught it early.

The cause turned out to be mundane. A dripline on that block-segment had a partial block from root growth, and was delivering maybe 40% of its intended flow to those rows. Caught then, it was a thirty-minute fix. Caught at veraison, it would have cost the grower quality points and brix uniformity across the block.

The lesson isn't the 970nm band

The 970nm trough is interesting mostly because of what it demonstrates. Plant stress is almost always detectable somewhere in the spectrum before it is visible on the surface. Water, nitrogen, chlorophyll breakdown, structural changes in cell walls — all of these have absorption signatures in the 400-2500nm range that a hyperspectral sensor can resolve.

The scout's job hasn't changed. What's changed is what the scout knows to look at, and when to look. Hyperspectral doesn't replace feet in the rows. It tells feet which rows.

Where we go from here

That Cab Franc block is now on continuous monitoring. We pull the Water Index every pass and the grower gets a ten-day lead on any repeat stress episode. Three dripline checks later, it's been a normal season. Whether it would have been a normal season without the satellite, we'll never know.

That's the thing about early detection. The reward is the absence of a bad story.
