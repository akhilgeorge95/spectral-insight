CANOPY · DETECTION MODEL

Four zones hiding inside one vineyard.

Canopy density is the classical use-case for satellite imagery. Hyperspectral adds the question every grower actually asks: why?

01 · THE SIGNAL

Density is easy. Cause is what matters.

Measuring canopy density from orbit is a solved problem — NDVI has done it for thirty years. Identifying vigor variation across a block will not surprise anyone who's driven the tractor there.

What's new is decomposing cause. Our model uses the full hyperspectral stack to tell you whether a low-vigor zone is low because of water stress, virus, nutrition, or because the soil simply changes under your feet. Four explanations, four different management responses.

02 · THE MODEL

k-means with a cause tag.

We cluster each block into four vigor zones using an unsupervised k-means on the hyperspectral feature vector. That clustering is purely descriptive — it tells you what varies, not why.

Then every zone is tagged with its dominant explanatory signal: water-driven, virus-suspect, nutrient-suspect, or soil/topography-driven (when none of the first three explain it). The tag sometimes matches the grower's intuition exactly. Sometimes it doesn't — those are the interesting ones.

We also correlate the zones against your harvest data if you share it. The zones that show up reliably in both vigor and yield are the real management zones, worth naming and tracking year over year.

04 · A REAL CASE

One vineyard. Four blocks in disguise.

A 9-hectare Twenty Mile Bench property had been managing their entire Pinot Noir block as a single unit for twelve years. Our first season of mapping revealed four stable zones, not one.

The south-facing corner was soil-driven high vigor (shallow clay, quicker warming). The north edge was water-driven low vigor (a drainage path). The centre had two zones: one virus-suspect (confirmed — GLRaV-3), one simply nutrient-deficient.

They didn't change the whole operation. They did change their sampling scheme, their selective spray timing, and their yield-estimation model. By year three the block's coefficient of variation on brix at harvest had dropped meaningfully.

05 · IN YOUR MONTHLY BRIEF

What you'll actually see.

  • Zone stability score — is this a real zone, or a one-pass artefact?
  • Yield-correlation overlay, if harvest data shared
06 · LIMITS

Things that break the clustering.

  • Very young plantings.

    Blocks under year four have too much variability inherent to vine establishment; our cause-decomposition is noisier.

  • Non-standard training.

    Heavily vertical shoot-positioned systems vs. sprawl systems read quite differently. We calibrate to the dominant regional systems; exotic setups get flagged.

  • Inter-row cover.

    If you've changed your inter-row cover-crop mid-season, the zones may shift. We can detect this but not yet fully separate it from the canopy itself.
