CLIMATE · DETECTION MODEL

Your topography, versus thirty years of Ontario weather.

This one isn't purely spectral. It's your elevation, aspect, and slope — extracted from the satellite stack — ranked against Ontario's climate record.

01 · THE SIGNAL

Not every risk is in a reflectance curve.

Some of the most important vineyard decisions — which blocks to protect from frost, which to prioritize for heat-spike irrigation, which to consider replanting — depend on topography, not spectral chemistry. Micro-site elevation, slope aspect, cold-air drainage pathways, exposure to prevailing wind.

We extract all of these from the satellite stack (which includes stereo-derived digital elevation) and score them against Ontario's 30-year climate record. The output is a forward-looking risk exposure rating per block, not a current-day detection. Useful at the season-planning scale, not the week-to-week scale.

02 · THE MODEL

Site vulnerability, ranked.

For each block, we compute a Late Frost Exposure Index, a Heat Spike Vulnerability Index, and a Wind Exposure rating. Each is scored against the climate record for the nearest three stations to your property, weighted by distance.

The output is a site-comparative ranking — not an absolute risk number, but 'block A is 2.3× more exposed than block C at this property.' That relative framing is what actually makes the protection decision tractable.

Used mostly at planning time: which blocks to site frost fans, which blocks to insure, which expansion parcels have hidden frost pockets.

04 · A REAL CASE

A hidden frost pocket, pre-plant.

A grower was considering a 6-hectare expansion parcel adjacent to their current property. It looked, visually, identical — same slope direction, same vegetation, same elevation on Google Earth.

Our exposure model showed it was in a subtle cold-air drainage path that ran through the existing neighbouring woodlot. The modelled late-frost risk on that parcel was 2.8× the highest-risk block on their current property.

They renegotiated the purchase price, and planted their most late-frost-hardy variety (Vidal Blanc) on that parcel instead of the originally planned Cabernet Franc. Four years in, they've dodged two frost events the rest of the region felt.

06 · LIMITS

Planning, not forecasting.

  • Not a weather forecast.

    This is exposure, not prediction. A high-exposure block can go a decade without an event; a low-exposure block can be hit in a 1-in-30-year year.

  • Requires adjacent data.

    In areas more than 25 km from a climate station, the baseline gets noisy. Fortunately, all three Ontario DVAs are well covered.

  • Doesn't capture canopy-height effects.

    Vineyard frost protection is also a function of trellis height and canopy management; those aren't in the topographic model and need to be judged separately.
